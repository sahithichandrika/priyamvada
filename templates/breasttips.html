Prevention and Treatment
Of
Breast Cancer

Prevention

There is no proven method of preventing breast cancer, however the risk of breast cancer can be reduced by lowering alcohol consumption and maintaining a healthy weight. Women who are at high risk because of a very strong family history may benefit from hormones such as tamoxifen, usually administered over five years. Bilateral prophylactic mastectomy can be considered in women at high risk of breast cancer due to gene mutations.

Treatments

  • Staging: Checks the size of the cancer and if it has spread. Uses CT and bone scans.
  • Surgery: Removes part or all of the breast. Types include lumpectomy (part) and mastectomy (whole). Often followed by radiotherapy.
  • Breast Prostheses and Reconstruction: Options to restore breast shape after surgery. Includes synthetic breasts worn in bras or surgical reconstruction.
  • Chemotherapy: Shrinks cancer before surgery or treats high-risk or recurring cancer. Useful for HER2 positive cancer.
  • Radiation Therapy: Destroys remaining cancer cells after surgery. Used after lumpectomy or if lymph nodes are removed.
  • Hormone Therapy: Reduces female hormones to slow hormone receptor-positive cancer. Type depends on age and menopause status.
  • Targeted Therapy: Drugs that attack specific cancer cell targets. Effective for HER2 positive cancer.
  • Palliative Care: Improves quality of life by alleviating symptoms. May include radiotherapy, chemotherapy, or other treatments.

Managing Side Effects

  • Lymphoedema: Swelling in the arm or breast due to lymph fluid buildup after lymph node removal.
  • Cording: Tight, cord-like structures in the arm after surgery. Gentle stretching can help.
  • Nerve Pain: Pain or pins and needles in the arm or chest after surgery. Chemotherapy can cause similar issues in hands and feet.
  • Memory and Thinking Changes: "Chemo brain" affects memory and thinking. Usually improves within a year.
  • Breast Prosthesis: Synthetic breast worn under clothing or in a bra after surgery.
  • Hair Loss: Common with chemotherapy and radiation. Other treatments may cause thinning.
  • Menopause and Fertility Changes: Chemotherapy can cause temporary or permanent menopause, affecting fertility. Discuss with your doctor if you want children.
